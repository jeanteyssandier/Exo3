Transit-Timing Variation Signature of Planet Migration:

The Case of K2-24

Jean Teyssandier & Anne-Sophie Libert (naXys, University of Namur)

Abstract
The convergent migration of two planets in a gaseous disc can lead to capture in mean motion resonance (MMR). We study the case of K2-24, a system of two planets whose period ratio indicates that they are in or near the 2:1 MMR, with non-zero eccentricities and large-amplitude transit-timing variations (TTV). By means of N-body simulations, we explore a scenario that can explain the architecture of this system. We propose a model in which capture in resonance occurs during migration in a disc with strong eccentricity damping, and is followed by eccentricity excitation during the dispersal of the disc, assisted by a third planet whose presence has been suggested by radial velocity observations. This model can account for the eccentricities of the two planets and their period ratio, as well as allowing us to reproduce the amplitude and period of the TTVs. It allows for a unified view of the formation and evolution history of K2-24, from disc-induced migration to its currently observed properties.

Introduction

  • Migration in a disc can lead to capture in mean motion resonances (MMR).
  • Planets in or near MMR produce a strong transit-timing variations (TTV) signal.
  • We study the signature of planet migration in the TTV signal of a pair of planets in the 2:1 MMR.
  • We use the K2-24 system as a benchmark.

K2-24

  • First reported by Petigura et al. (2016), then refined with TTVs by Petigura et al. (2018).
  • Two planets at 20.9 and 42.3 days (period ratio 2.028), with masses 19 and \(15 M_{\oplus}\) , and non-zero eccentricities.
  • TTV period of 1580 days, and amplitude of 0.25 and 0.40 days.
  • Possible third planet: \(54 M_{\oplus}\) at 1.15 au.

Fig.1: Eccentricities (top) and TTVs (bottom) of K2-24, from Petigura et al. (2018).

Model

  • We use the Rebound code, with extra forces to simulate disc-driven migration and eccentricity damping.
  • We use the Kanagawa and Szuszkiewicz (2020) model for the damping terms.
  • We use the work of Lithwick et al. (2012) to interpret TTVs.

Increasing the eccentricities and TTVs

  • The eccentricities (and TTV amplitudes) that we obtained with disc migration are too small.
  • Petigura et al. (2018) reported the possible detection of a third planet (\(54 M_{\oplus}\) at 1.15 au)
  • We assume that all three planets have cleared an inner cavity and or no longer migrating.
  • However the gravitational torque from the disc still causes the planets to precess ( see Teyssandier and Lai 2019 for details).
  • As the disc evaporates over time, change in precession rates cause crossing of secular resonances, and hence eccentricity excitation ( see Lithwick and Wu 2011 for details).

• This mechanism reproduces all the observed properties of K2-24 (orbital period ratios, eccentricities, TTVs).

Conclusion

  • Disc migration leaves an inprint in the TTVs of resonant pairs of planets.
  • Disc migration alone could not reproduce the TTVs of K2-24, although it could reproduce its orbital period ratio.
  • We found a possible scenario where a third planet and an evporating disc excite the eccentricities and reproduce the right TTV amplitude, period, and the orbital period.
  • If this scenario really happened, then it predicts that the putative third planet indeed exists, and is moderately eccentric (~0.25).
